# XX — Libyan Sands: Travel in a Dead World

### *Ralph Bagnold*

**"The desert is a giant laboratory where the wind is the master and the sand is the plaything of physics."** — *Ralph Bagnold*

### **[ The Essentials ]**

* **Genre:** Travel Memoir / Scientific Exploration
* **Sub-Genre:** Automotive Pioneering
* **Keywords:** *The Sahara, Egypt, Libya, Model T Ford, Sand Dunes, Physics, Pre-WWII*
* **Traveler Profile:** **The Engineer Explorer.** You see a vast, empty desert and your first thought isn't "how beautiful," but "how do I drive a car across that without dying?"

---

### **[ The Author: Ralph Bagnold (1896–1990) ]**

Ralph Bagnold was a British soldier, geomorphologist, and the man who literally wrote the book on how sand moves. Long before he founded the **Long Range Desert Group** (the precursor to the SAS) during WWII, he was a young officer stationed in Egypt with a thirst for the "deep" desert. Bagnold was the opposite of the romantic, flowing-robed explorer. He was a man of cold logic, mechanical ingenuity, and relentless curiosity. He spent his leaves in the 1920s and 30s stripping down Ford cars and venturing into the "Dead World" of the Libyan Desert, a place so arid that even the Bedouin avoided it.

### **[ The Book: *Libyan Sands* ]**

First published in 1935, this is a chronicle of the "mechanical age" of exploration. It documents a series of expeditions into the Great Sand Sea and the Gilf Kebir.

The writing is direct, modest, and intellectually sharp. Bagnold treats the desert like a puzzle. He uses small paragraphs to explain the logistics of "sun-compass" navigation and the invention of **sand channels**—metal strips used to stop tires from sinking. He has a dramatic flair for the silence of the desert, which he describes as "dead" because of the total absence of life, water, or even the sound of insects.

He provides colorful detail about the physics of the dunes. He was the first to realize that sand dunes aren't just piles of dirt; they are organized structures that move and "sing" according to mathematical laws.

Bagnold captures the thrill of reaching "The Zerzura Oasis" (or finding it was a myth) and discovering ancient rock art in places no human had seen for thousands of years. It is a story of a man who looked at a wasteland and found a world of profound order and beauty.

---

### **[ Destinations Explored ]**

* **The Great Sand Sea:** A massive expanse of dunes on the border of Egypt and Libya that Bagnold was the first to cross by motor car.
* **The Gilf Kebir:** A vast sandstone plateau where he discovered "The Lost Oasis" of folklore was actually a series of hidden valleys.
* **Siwa Oasis:** The legendary home of the Oracle of Ammon, used as a jumping-off point for his deeper forays.
* **Uweinat:** A mountain rising from the flat desert where he found prehistoric paintings of cattle, proving the Sahara was once green.

### **[ Further Reading ]**

**By Bagnold:**

* ***The Physics of Blown Sand and Desert Dunes***: The scientific bible of the desert; it’s still used by NASA today to study Mars.

**In a Similar Vein:**

* ***The English Patient*** by Michael Ondaatje: A fictionalized (and highly romanticized) version of the desert explorers of Bagnold's era.
* ***The Lost Oasis*** by Saul Kelly: A historical account of the real-life "Zerzura Club" to which Bagnold belonged.
* ***The Long Range Desert Group*** by W.B. Kennedy Shaw: An account of how Bagnold's desert navigation skills were used to fight the Nazis.
