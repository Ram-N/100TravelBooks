# XX — Tracks

### *One Woman’s Journey Across the Australian Outback*

**"I believe that the self is an expendable luxury."** — *Robyn Davidson*

---

### **[ The Essentials ]**

* **Genre:** Travel Memoir
* **Sub-Genre:** Adventure / Solo Expedition
* **Keywords:** *Australia, Camels, Desert, Solitude, Feminism, Aboriginal Culture, Alice Springs*
* **Traveler Profile:** **The Lone Wolf.** You believe that the only way to find yourself is to get lost in a place that doesn't care if you live or die.

---

### **[ The Author: Robyn Davidson (1950–Present) ]**

Robyn Davidson is an Australian writer who became a reluctant icon. In 1977 she decided to walk 1,700 miles across the harsh Western Australian desert. She didn't do it for fame or a book deal. She did it because she wanted to see if she could survive on her own terms. She spent two years in Alice Springs just learning how to train wild camels. She is a woman of fierce independence and a sharp, unsentimental intellect. She writes with a raw honesty that cuts through the romantic myths of the "outback."

### **[ The Book: *Tracks* ]**

This is a story of grit and sun-baked madness. Davidson sets out from Alice Springs headed for the Indian Ocean. Her companions are four temperamental camels—Zeleika, Dookie, Bub, and Goliath—and a loyal dog named Diggity. She carries everything she needs on the camels' backs. She walks through some of the most inhospitable terrain on the planet.

The writing is vivid and dramatic. Davidson describes the physical toll of the journey—the blistered skin and the constant thirst and the sheer exhaustion. But the real journey is internal. She writes about the "desert madness" that sets in when you are alone for months. She battles her own fears and the sexism of the men she meets along the way. She has a colorful way of describing her camels. They are not just pack animals; they are complex, difficult, and beloved friends.

She also explores the deep injustice faced by the Aboriginal people. She spends time with an elder named Mr. Eddie who teaches her how to see the land not as a void but as a map of stories. She is a master of the small, punchy paragraph. She makes you feel the silence of the desert and the intrusion of the *National Geographic* photographer who occasionally checks in on her. It is a book about stripping away the layers of civilization until only the "tracks" remain.

### **[ Destinations Explored ]**

* **Alice Springs:** The rough-and-tumble town where she spent years preparing for the impossible.
* **The Gibson Desert:** A vast and punishing landscape of red sand and spinifex grass.
* **The Indian Ocean:** The final, blue horizon where the long walk finally ends in the surf.
* **Aboriginal Settlements:** Places where she witnessed the clash between ancient tradition and modern neglect.

---

### **[ Further Reading ]**

**By Davidson:**

* ***Desert Places***: Her account of traveling with the nomadic Rabari people in India.
* ***Quarterly Essay: No Fixed Address***: Her reflections on nomadism and the modern world.

**In a Similar Vein:**

* ***The Lost Continent*** by Bill Bryson: A much funnier and less dangerous look at traveling across a vast land.
* ***The Songlines*** by Bruce Chatwin: A classic (though controversial) exploration of Aboriginal culture and the philosophy of walking.

### **[ The Signature Line ]**

> "The two important things I did learn were that you are as powerful and strong as you allow yourself to be."

### **[ The Perfect Pairing ]**

* **The Drink:** A mouthful of lukewarm water from a canteen that tastes of metal and dust.
* **The Soundtrack:** The rhythmic shuffling of camel hooves and the whistling of the wind through dead wood.
* **The Setting:** A sleeping bag under a sky so full of stars that it feels like it might collapse on you.
