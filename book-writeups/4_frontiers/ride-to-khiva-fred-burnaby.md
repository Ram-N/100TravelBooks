# XX — A Ride to Khiva

### *Fred Burnaby*

**"The Russian government may close the borders, but they cannot close the road to a man who has a good horse and a stubborn mind."** — *Fred Burnaby*

---

### **[ The Essentials ]**

* **Genre:** Travel Memoir / Military Adventure
* **Sub-Genre:** The Great Game
* **Keywords:** *Central Asia, Uzbekistan, Russian Empire, Horseback, Winter, 1875, Forbidden Cities*
* **Traveler Profile:** **The Victorian Daredevil.** You are a fan of "Flashman" style heroics, but with the added weight of real history and a man who could actually carry a pony under his arm.

---

### **[ The Author: Fred Burnaby (1842–1885) ]**

Captain Fred Burnaby was the ultimate Victorian "Action Man." Standing six-foot-four and weighing over 250 pounds, he was a giant in an era of small men. He was an officer in the Royal Horse Guards, a balloonist, a linguist, and a man who lived for the thrill of the "forbidden." In 1875, he heard that the Russian Empire had closed the Khanate of Khiva to all foreigners as they pushed their borders toward British India. Burnaby took it as a personal challenge. Without official permission from his superiors, he packed a set of Cockle's pills, a heavy coat, and set off into the sub-zero winter of the Central Asian steppes.

### **[ The Book: *A Ride to Khiva* ]**

This is the quintessential account of "The Great Game"—the shadow war between Britain and Russia for control of Asia. Burnaby's narrative is a mix of high-stakes espionage and brutal survival.

The writing is direct, punchy, and incredibly conversational. Burnaby doesn't waste time on flowery descriptions of his feelings. He focuses on the practicalities: how to avoid frostbite when the mercury freezes in the thermometer, how to outwit Russian spies, and how to deal with the constant greed of his guides. He uses small paragraphs to keep the action moving, often jumping from a political debate about the "Russian Menace" to a colorful description of his evening meal.

He provides colorful detail about the people of the steppe. He describes the Kirghiz and Tatar tribes with a mix of soldierly respect and Victorian condescension. He has a dramatic flair for the absurdity of his situation—an English gentleman drinking tea with a Khan while Russian authorities are scouring the desert to arrest him and send him home. It is a story of sheer physical endurance and the kind of "amateur" diplomacy that changed the maps of the world.

---

### **[ Destinations Explored ]**

* **St. Petersburg:** The glittering starting point where he began his cat-and-mouse game with Russian bureaucracy.
* **The Kirghiz Steppe:** A frozen wasteland of snow and wind where his horse was his only friend.
* **The Aral Sea:** A desolate, ice-choked body of water that marked the halfway point of his journey.
* **Khiva:** The forbidden, walled city of the desert. A place of blue domes, narrow alleys, and a Khan who was fascinated by the giant Englishman.

### **[ Further Reading ]**

**By Burnaby:**

* ***On Horseback Through Asia Minor***: His later account of a 2,000-mile ride through Turkey and Armenia.

**In a Similar Vein:**

* ***The Great Game*** by Peter Hopkirk: The definitive history of the British-Russian rivalry that Burnaby was a part of.
* ***News from Tartary*** by Peter Fleming: A 20th-century echo of Burnaby’s journey, featuring another dry-witted Englishman on a long road.
* ***Setting the Desert on Fire*** by James Barr: For more on the strategic struggles for the Middle East and Central Asia.
