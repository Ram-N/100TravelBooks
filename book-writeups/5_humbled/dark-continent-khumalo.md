# XX — Dark Continent, My Black Arse

### *Sihle Khumalo*

**"I am an African. I am not a tourist. I am just a man trying to see his own backyard."** — *Sihle Khumalo*

---

### **[ The Essentials ]**

* **Genre:** Travel Memoir
* **Sub-Genre:** Contemporary African Travel
* **Keywords:** *Africa, Cape to Cairo, Public Transport, Post-Colonialism, Humour, Identity*
* **Traveler Profile:** **The No-Nonsense Nomad.** You want the truth about a continent, served with a side of dry wit and a total lack of ego.

---

### **[ The Author: Sihle Khumalo (1975–Present) ]**

Sihle Khumalo was born in South Africa and he worked in a corporate job before he decided to blow his savings on a dream. He is not a professional explorer with a film crew. He is a guy from the township who realized he knew more about London than he did about Lusaka. He has a sharp tongue and a brilliant eye for the ridiculous. He refuses to look at Africa through the "poverty porn" lens of Western media or the romantic haze of the "Lion King" fans. He is direct, funny, and very real.

### **[ The Book: *Dark Continent, My Black Arse* ]**

The title says it all. Khumalo is tired of the clichés. On his thirtieth birthday, he quit his job, grabbed a backpack, and decided to travel from Cape Town to Cairo using only public transport. No 4x4s. No private planes. Just sweaty buses, crowded trains, and the occasional terrifying taxi ride.

The writing is fast-paced and colloquial. Khumalo talks to you like you are sitting next to him on a long bus ride. He describes the "border-post blues" and the endless paperwork of moving between countries. He writes about the "scramble for Africa" in a modern sense—the scramble to find a working toilet or a cold beer. He has a dramatic flair for the absurd situations he finds himself in, like being stuck in a town with no money and a bus that might never leave.

He explores the ghost of Cecil Rhodes and the reality of modern dictators. He looks at the ruins of Great Zimbabwe and the pyramids of Giza. But mostly, he looks at the people. He meets pastors, hustlers, and weary travelers. He asks hard questions about why things don't work, but he also celebrates the incredible energy and rhythm of the continent. He shows that Africa is not a "dark" mystery. it is a vibrant, messy, and loud place that belongs to the people living in it.

This book is a breath of fresh air. It breaks down the walls between "African" and "traveler." Khumalo shows that you don't need a pith helmet to explore the world. You just need a bit of courage and a very thick skin.

### **[ Destinations Explored ]**

* **Harare:** A city of vanished dreams and stubborn survival.
* **Zanzibar:** The spice island where he tries to find the romance amidst the tourist traps.
* **Addis Ababa:** The bustling, mountain capital where he grapples with history and bad food.
* **Cairo:** The chaotic finish line where the Nile finally meets the sea.

---

### **[ Further Reading ]**

**By Khumalo:**

* ***Heart of Africa***: His journey through West Africa, including Nigeria and Ghana.
* ***Almost Sleeping My Way to Timbuktu***: Another hilarious and honest trek across the continent.

**In a Similar Vein:**

* ***The Shadow of the Sun*** by Ryszard Kapuściński: A more serious but equally deep look at the continent’s soul.
* ***Blood River*** by Tim Butcher: A gritty, dangerous trek through the Congo.
