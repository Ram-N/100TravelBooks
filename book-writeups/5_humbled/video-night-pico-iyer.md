# Video Night in Kathmandu

### *And Other Reports from the Not-So-Far East*

**"American dreams are strongest in the hearts of those who have seen America only in their dreams."** — *Pico Iyer*

### **[ The Author: Pico Iyer (b. 1957) ]**

Born in Oxford to Indian parents and educated at Eton, Oxford, and Harvard, Pico Iyer embodies the very cultural hybridity he writes about. His name itself is a multinational artifact: Siddharth (after the Buddha), Pico (after Renaissance philosopher Pico della Mirandola), Raghavan Iyer. Raised between England, India, and California, he became a *Time* magazine writer in 1982, covering world affairs with the sensibility of someone perpetually betweenness—neither tourist nor native, always the observant outsider. His debut, *Video Night in Kathmandu*, emerged from a leave of absence when he traveled through Asia in the mid-1980s, witnessing the first wave of American pop culture's tsunami across the continent. Since 1992, he has lived in Nara, Japan, with his Japanese wife, though he spends part of each year at a Benedictine hermitage in California. Iyer has since published fifteen books exploring displacement, globalization, and stillness, becoming one of the most philosophically minded travel writers of his generation. His TED Talks have been viewed millions of times, and he remains a regular contributor to *The New York Review of Books*, writing with the precision of a journalist and the soul of a poet.

### **[ The Book: *Video Night in Kathmandu* ]**

Published in 1988, this collection of eleven essays captured something brand new: the bizarre, poignant, and often hilarious collision between ancient Asian cultures and American pop imperialism. Iyer arrived in Kathmandu expecting hippies and hash, Tibet and transcendence. Instead, he found Rambo playing on loop, Led Zeppelin blasting from temples, and restaurants serving dishes named "Ike and Tuna Turner." The book ranges across ten countries—Nepal, India, Burma, Hong Kong, Philippines, Thailand, China, Tibet, Bali, and Japan—documenting a moment when Asia was absorbing Western culture with dizzying speed while Western travelers were searching for authenticity that had already vanished.

The prose sparkles with wit and irony. Iyer writes about five Bollywood Rambo knockoffs in Bombay (one starring a woman in a sari), baseball mania in Tokyo, sex tourism in Bangkok, yuppies in Hong Kong who pick up dates by asking "What do you think of the dollar?" He has a gift for the absurd detail: Nepalese peasants in Madonna T-shirts, Communist guerrillas in UCLA gear, the deliberate misspellings of American menu items in China. But beneath the humor lies serious cultural analysis. Who is colonizing whom? When does cultural exchange become cultural erasure? Is this globalization or Americanization?

What prevents the book from being merely a catalog of amusing juxtapositions is Iyer's refusal to judge. He resists easy conclusions about corruption or contamination. Sometimes the West is doing the hustling; sometimes it's being hustled. Sometimes ancient cultures absorb pop culture and remain essentially unchanged; sometimes they're genuinely transformed. The writing style blends travel journalism with philosophical meditation, influenced by his background straddling multiple worlds. At just over three decades old, *Video Night in Kathmandu* now reads as both dated (the Asia of the 1980s is utterly gone) and prescient (it predicted the globalized world we now inhabit). It remains a landmark in travel literature—the book that showed the "final frontier" wasn't geographic but cultural.

### **[ Destinations Explored ]**

* **Kathmandu, Nepal:** Where the Dharma path meets Freak Street, and Westerners seeking enlightenment encounter locals seeking Levi's.
* **Tokyo, Japan:** A nation equally obsessed with baseball, sumo, and Disneyland—tradition and pop culture coexisting in surreal harmony.
* **Bombay (Mumbai), India:** Bollywood reimagining Hollywood, where Rambo becomes Rani (Queen) in a sari, complete with dance numbers.
* **Bali, Indonesia:** The island paradise where mohawk haircuts and gamelan music share the beach.
* **Bangkok, Thailand:** Sex tourism capital and spiritual crossroads, Madonna in the massage parlors, Buddha in the temples.

---

### **[ Further Reading ]**

**By Pico Iyer:**

* ***The Lady and the Monk***: Iyer's immersion in Kyoto, falling in love with a Japanese woman while living in a Zen monastery—a deeper dive into one culture.
* ***The Global Soul***: His millennial meditation on jet lag, airports, and belonging nowhere—the intellectual sequel to *Video Night*.

**In a Similar Vein:**

* ***The Great Railway Bazaar*** by Paul Theroux: The godfather of witty, acerbic travel writing through Asia—an obvious influence on Iyer's style.
* ***Nothing to Declare*** by Mary Morris: Crossing Latin America as a woman in the 1980s, with similar attention to cultural collision and irony.

### **[ The Signature Line ]**

> "The New Yorker disappoints the locals by turning into a barefoot ascetic dressed in bangles and beads, while the Nepali peasant frustrates his foreign supplicants by turning out to be a traveling salesman in Levi's and Madonna T-shirt. Soon, neither is quite the person he was, or the one the other wanted."

### **[ The Perfect Pairing ]**

* **The Drink:** A mango lassi from a roadside stand with a Michael Jackson poster on the wall—perfectly capturing the book's hybrid spirit.
* **The Soundtrack:** An iTunes shuffle between sitar music, Rambo's theme, and Japanese J-pop—let the contradictions wash over you.
* **The Setting:** An international airport terminal at 3 AM, that liminal nowhere-space between cultures where everything and nothing makes sense.
