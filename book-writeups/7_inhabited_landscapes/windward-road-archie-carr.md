# XX — The Windward Road

### *Archie Carr*

**"The world is a very old place, and the sea turtles have seen more of it than we ever will."** — *Archie Carr*

### **[ The Essentials ]**

* **Genre:** Travel Memoir / Natural History
* **Sub-Genre:** Scientific Adventure
* **Keywords:** *Caribbean, Sea Turtles, Costa Rica, Tortuguero, Ecology, 1950s, Conservation*
* **Traveler Profile:** **The Naturalist.** You are a person who prefers the company of ancient reptiles to people and believes that a beach at midnight is the most exciting place on Earth.

---

### **[ The Author: Archie Carr (1909–1987) ]**

Archie Carr was a legendary herpetologist and a professor at the University of Florida. He wasn't just a man in a lab coat. He was a writer with the soul of a poet and the stamina of a field hand. In the 1950s, he set out to solve a biological mystery. He wanted to know where the great green sea turtles went to nest. He was a man of immense curiosity and a very dry, Southern wit. He didn't just study nature; he advocated for it. He became the voice of the sea turtle, and his work eventually led to the creation of the Tortuguero National Park.

### **[ The Book: *The Windward Road* ]**

This is the book that launched the modern sea turtle conservation movement. It is a collection of stories about Carr's travels through the Caribbean—from the Bahamas to the "Wild Coast" of Costa Rica. He was looking for a "mythical" nesting beach that he had only heard about in whispers.

The writing is direct and incredibly scannable. Carr describes the "windward road" as the path where the trade winds blow and the sea turtles roam. He uses small paragraphs to describe the people he met along the way—turtle hunters, beachcombers, and local legends. He has a dramatic flair for the midnight walks on the beach. He describes the sight of a massive green turtle emerging from the surf like something from a prehistoric dream.

He provides colorful detail about the smells of the salt spray and the sound of the jungle at night. But there is a darkness to the book, too. Carr saw that the turtles were being hunted to extinction. He wrote about the "turtle crawls" where the animals were kept upside down in the sun before being turned into soup. His prose is so powerful that it turned readers into activists. It is a story about a man falling in love with a species and then realizing he has to fight to save it.

---

### **[ Destinations Explored ]**

* **Tortuguero, Costa Rica:** The "Place of the Turtles." A remote stretch of black sand beach that Carr made famous.
* **The Cayman Islands:** Once the center of the world's turtle trade, now a place of memory and conservation.
* **Trinidad and the Grenadines:** Where he studied the nesting habits of the leatherbacks and the hawksbills.
* **The Open Sea:** Which Carr describes as a "vast, blue desert" that the turtles navigate with mysterious precision.

---

### **[ Further Reading ]**

**By Carr:**

* ***So Excellent a Fishe***: A deeper look into the biology and migrations of the green turtle.
* ***High Jungles and Low***: His account of life and research in the forests of Honduras.

**In a Similar Vein:**

* ***The Log from the Sea of Cortez*** by John Steinbeck: For another classic blend of marine biology and travel philosophy.
* ***Voyage of the Turtle*** by Carl Safina: A modern update on the state of the world's sea turtles and the legacy of Archie Carr.
