# XX — One Day I Will Write About This Place

### *Binyavanga Wainaina*

**"I am a creature of the middle. A creature of the between."** — *Binyavanga Wainaina*

---

### **[ The Essentials ]**

* **Genre:** Memoir
* **Sub-Genre:** Coming-of-Age / Intellectual Biography
* **Keywords:** *Kenya, Pan-Africanism, Language, Identity, Music, Transformation, Nairobi*
* **Traveler Profile:** **The Cultural Insider.** You want to see Africa not as a destination, but as a complex, modern, and deeply sonic experience.

---

### **[ The Author: Binyavanga Wainaina (1971–2019) ]**

Binyavanga Wainaina was a titan of modern African letters. He was a Kenyan writer and journalist who famously won the Caine Prize and founded the influential literary magazine *Kwani?*. He was a man who hated clichés. He gained global fame for his satirical essay "How to Write About Africa," where he mocked the Western habit of filling African stories with sunsets and starving children. Binyavanga was a visionary who championed a new, bold, and queer African identity. He wrote with a frantic, electric energy that mirrored the pulse of the continent he loved.

### **[ The Book: *One Day I Will Write About This Place* ]**

This is not a traditional, linear autobiography. It is a sensory explosion. Binyavanga takes us from his middle-class childhood in Nakuru to his university days in South Africa and his return to a changing Kenya. He doesn't just tell you what happened; he makes you hear it. He tracks the history of his life through the sounds of the time—the pop songs on the radio, the shifting dialects of the streets, and the political speeches that shaped the nation.

The writing is incredibly rhythmic and colorful. Binyavanga uses language like a painter uses a brush. He describes the "glitchy" feeling of being a teenager and the way different African identities clash and merge. He has a dramatic flair for capturing the internal chaos of a young man trying to find his voice. He uses small paragraphs and sharp, jagged sentences that pull you along at a breathless pace.

The book explores his family’s roots in Kenya and Uganda. He looks at the complex tribal politics of his homeland without falling into easy answers. He writes about the "lost years" in South Africa where he struggled with depression and found his calling as a writer. This is a story about the birth of an artist. It is about how a person becomes a writer by learning to see the world exactly as it is, rather than how others expect it to be.

This memoir is a landmark. It is a defiant and beautiful claim to a modern African identity that is urban, intellectual, and deeply personal. Binyavanga shows us that "this place" is not a static background but a living, breathing entity that changes every time someone dares to write about it.

### **[ Destinations Explored ]**

* **Nakuru:** The town of his childhood, filled with the middle-class comforts and anxieties of a post-independence Kenya.
* **Nairobi:** The high-speed, multi-lingual heart of East Africa where the future is always being invented.
* **Transkei, South Africa:** The lonely, wind-swept landscape of his university years and his personal "wilderness."
* **Lome, Togo:** A brief, vivid excursion that expands his vision of what it means to be African.

---

### **[ Further Reading ]**

**By Wainaina:**

* ***How to Write About Africa***: The essential satirical essay that every traveler to the continent should read.
* ***I Am a Homosexual, Mum***: A courageous "lost chapter" of his memoir published later in his life.

**In a Similar Vein:**

* ***Nairobi Heat*** by Mukoma Wa Ngugi: A gritty crime novel that captures the darker side of the Kenyan capital.
* ***Dust*** by Yvonne Adhiambo Owuor: A powerful novel that explores the hidden histories and silences of Kenya.
