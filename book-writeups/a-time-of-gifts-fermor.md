# XX — A Time of Gifts

### *On Foot to Constantinople*

**"I was eighteen and the continent lay there before me, covered with a network of red lines, the walking of which would fill a lifetime."** — *Patrick Leigh Fermor*  

---

### **[ The Essentials ]**

* **Genre:** Continental Walking Memoir  
* **Sub-Genre:** Pre‑War Europe, Literary Travel  
* **Keywords:** *Danube, youth, aristocracies, Nazism, rivers, castles, notebooks, Mittel-Europa*  
* **Traveler Profile:** **The Erudite Wanderer.** For readers who want their journeys on foot laced with languages, history, and baroque digressions, and who enjoy the tension between youthful naivety and seasoned hindsight.  

---

### **[ The Author: Patrick Leigh Fermor (1915–2011) ]**

Patrick Leigh Fermor was an English travel writer, soldier, and polymath, often hailed as one of the greatest travel stylists of the 20th century. Expelled from school as a teenager, he set out in December 1933 to walk from the Hook of Holland to Constantinople, carrying little more than a rucksack, a couple of books, and letters of introduction. That journey, interrupted by nights in barns, monasteries, and the houses of Central European aristocrats, later became the basis for his celebrated trilogy beginning with *A Time of Gifts*. During the Second World War he served with British special forces in occupied Greece, helping to organize the Cretan resistance and famously abducting a German general. After the war he settled between Greece and England, publishing travel books such as *The Traveller’s Tree*, *Mani*, and *Roumeli*, and becoming a touchstone for writers drawn to slow, observant travel and richly textured prose.  

---

### **[ The Book: *A Time of Gifts* ]**

*A Time of Gifts* covers the first stage of Leigh Fermor’s epic walk from the Hook of Holland to Constantinople, written four decades after the journey but grounded in the notebooks he kept at the time. Beginning in winter 1933, the 18‑year‑old crosses the Netherlands and Germany and follows the Rhine and Danube through Austria and into Hungary, sleeping in youth hostels, barns, inns, and, thanks to letters of introduction, in a surprising number of grand houses and schlosses. The route carries him from frosty dykes and barges to baroque churches and candlelit dining rooms, and from student taverns to monastery cells, all under the lengthening shadow of Nazism and impending war.  

What distinguishes the book is the interplay between youthful experience and the older writer’s reflective voice. Leigh Fermor describes landscapes, architecture, and people with an almost baroque density of metaphor and allusion, moving easily between peasant folklore, classical literature, and contemporary politics. The tone is frequently exuberant and curious—he is intoxicated by libraries, uniforms, dialects, and old rituals—but threaded with elegy, because he knows that much of the world he is walking through will soon be bombed, occupied, or swept away. As a result, *A Time of Gifts* reads not simply as a travelogue but as a portrait of a vanished Europe, and as a record of how a restless teenager grows into a writer by walking, observing, and reading his way across a continent.  

---

### **[ Destinations Explored ]**

* **Rotterdam and the Dutch lowlands:** Canal‑laced, wind‑scoured landscapes that provide the journey’s damp, wintry launchpad.  
* **The Rhine valley in Germany:** A corridor of barges, castles, and riverside towns where rising Nazi pageantry jostles with medieval survivals.  
* **Cologne, Bonn, and Munich:** University cities and cultural centers where he encounters both bohemian circles and the new political mood.  
* **The Upper Danube through Austria:** Monasteries, baroque churches, and riverside villages that anchor some of the book’s most detailed set‑pieces.  
* **The Hungarian plain and Budapest:** A transition toward the Balkans, with great houses, steppe horizons, and hints of the more tumultuous East to come.  

---

### **[ Further Reading ]**

**By Patrick Leigh Fermor:**  

* ***Between the Woods and the Water***: The second volume of the journey, carrying him from the Middle Danube through Transylvania and toward the Iron Gates.  
* ***The Broken Road***: The posthumously assembled final volume, taking the walk on through Bulgaria and down to the shores of the Black Sea.  

**In a Similar Vein:**  

* ***The Road to Oxiana*** by Robert Byron: A 1930s journey through the Middle East and Persia, matching Leigh Fermor’s erudition with a more spiky, modernist voice.  
* ***The Traveller’s Tree*** by Patrick Leigh Fermor: His earlier post‑war exploration of the Caribbean, blending history, anthropology, and lush descriptive writing.

### **[ The Signature Line ]**

> The pages suggest that the true subject of the journey is not simply a line across Europe, but the way a young mind absorbs languages, landscapes, and histories just before the storm breaks.  

---

### **[ The Perfect Pairing ]**

* **The Drink:** A glass of robust Central European red wine or dark beer, echoing the taverns and castle dining rooms along the Rhine and Danube.  
* **The Soundtrack:** Quiet classical strings or solo piano—Bach, Mozart, or Schubert—to match the book’s reflective, historically attentive mood.  
* **The Setting:** A winter evening armchair with a blanket and a detailed map of Europe, so you can trace his route from sea‑level dykes to snow‑dusted river valleys.
