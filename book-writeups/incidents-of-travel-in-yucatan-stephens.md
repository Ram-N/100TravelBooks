### *John Lloyd Stephens*

### **[ The Author: John Lloyd Stephens (1805–1852) ]**

John Lloyd Stephens was an American diplomat, lawyer, and adventurer who effectively "discovered" the Maya civilization for the Western world. Before Stephens, many believed the great ruins of Central America were built by Egyptians or lost tribes of Israel. Stephens, along with his indispensable partner, the English artist **Frederick Catherwood**, set out to prove they were the work of indigenous Americans. Stephens was a gifted storyteller with a legal mind for detail and a dry, self-deprecating wit. He didn't just find ruins; he bought them—famously purchasing the entire city of Copán for fifty dollars.

### **[ The Book: *Incidents of Travel in Yucatán* ]**

Published in 1843, this is the sequel to his best-selling *Incidents of Travel in Central America, Chiapas, and Yucatan*. This specific volume focuses on their second, more detailed expedition through the northern Yucatán Peninsula, visiting over forty Maya sites.

The writing is direct, lively, and filled with dramatic flair. Stephens describes the grueling reality of 19th-century travel: the "garrapatas" (ticks), the yellow fever, and the perpetual struggle to find clean water. He uses small paragraphs to break down complex architectural observations, often contrasting the scale of the ruins with the humble villages nearby.

A defining feature of the book is the synergy between Stephens' prose and **Catherwood’s illustrations**. Catherwood used a *camera lucida* to achieve near-photographic accuracy in his drawings of hieroglyphs and carvings.

Stephens provides colorful detail about the "hacienda" lifestyle and the politics of Mexico at the time. He captures the awe of standing before the "Pyramid of the Magician" at Uxmal while it was still half-buried in forest. It is a story of a man who realized he was looking at a history that had been forgotten by the world, and he felt a desperate urge to document it before the jungle reclaimed it entirely.

### **[ Destinations Explored ]**

* **Uxmal:** The site that most captivated Stephens, where he spent weeks documenting the "Governor’s House."
* **Chichén Itzá:** Where he marveled at the "Castillo" and the vast ball court.
* **Kabah & Sayil:** Lesser-known sites where he discovered the famous "arch" and incredible stone masks.
* **Mérida:** The colonial capital that served as their base of operations and a window into 19th-century Yucatecan society.

### **[ Further Reading ]**

**By Stephens:**

* ***Incidents of Travel in Central America, Chiapas, and Yucatan***: The first part of the journey, covering Copán and Palenque.

**In a Similar Vein:**

* ***Jungle of Stone*** by William Carlsen: A modern, dual biography of Stephens and Catherwood and their epic discoveries.
* ***The Lost City of the Monkey God*** by Douglas Preston: For a modern-day hunt for ruins in the same spirit of adventure.
* ***Catherwood's Views of Ancient Monuments***: A book dedicated solely to the stunning lithographs that accompanied Stephens' text.
