# XX — Iron and Silk

### *Mark Salzman*

**"I went to China to teach English, but I was the one who went to school."** — *Mark Salzman*

### **[ The Essentials ]**

* **Genre:** Memoir / Travel
* **Sub-Genre:** Martial Arts / Cultural Exchange
* **Keywords:** *China, Kung Fu, Wushu, Pan Qingfu, 1980s, Calligraphy, Teacher*
* **Traveler Profile:** **The Dedicated Apprentice.** You are someone who believes that the best way to understand a culture is to let it beat the ego out of you in a training hall.

---

### **[ The Author: Mark Salzman (1959–Present) ]**

Mark Salzman was a young Yale graduate who arrived in China in 1982. This was a pivotal moment. The Cultural Revolution was over and the country was just beginning to open its doors to the West. Salzman was a "foreign expert" hired to teach English. But he wasn't just a scholar. He was a lifelong martial arts enthusiast and a cellist. He was a man of immense curiosity and a self-deprecating wit. He didn't want to live in a gilded cage for expats. He wanted to find the "real" China—the one made of ancient traditions and iron-hard discipline.

### **[ The Book: *Iron and Silk* ]**

This is a collection of vignettes about his two years in Changsha. It is a portrait of a society in transition. The "Iron" refers to the martial arts and the physical toughness of the people. The "Silk" refers to the calligraphy, the music, and the delicate social graces that survived decades of turmoil.

The writing is direct and very conversational. Salzman uses small paragraphs to capture the absurdity and the beauty of his daily life. He describes his struggle to master the Chinese language and the oddities of being a "giant" in a land of small people. He has a dramatic flair for the training sequences. He eventually became a student of Pan Qingfu, one of the greatest martial arts masters in China. Pan was known as "Iron Fist." He was a man who could punch through a brick and he had no patience for lazy students.

The book is filled with colorful details about his students and his neighbors. He writes about the "back door" culture where everything from lightbulbs to train tickets required a personal connection. He describes the intense pressure his students felt to succeed and the quiet ways they showed him affection. He doesn't lecture the reader on politics. He just shows the human cost and the human triumph of living in a Communist state.

It is a story about the bridge between two worlds. Salzman shows that even when languages and ideologies are completely different, people can find common ground through a shared love of craft. Whether it is a perfectly executed kick or a beautifully written character, excellence is a universal language.

### **[ Destinations Explored ]**

* **Changsha:** The gritty, industrial capital of Hunan Province where he lived and taught.
* **The Training Hall:** The spartan room where he bled and sweated under the watchful eye of Master Pan.
* **The Night Markets:** Where he learned the true meaning of Chinese "street food" and social interaction.

### **[ Further Reading ]**

**By Salzman:**

* ***The Soloist***: A novel about a cello prodigy that echoes his own musical background.
* ***Lost in Place***: A memoir about his eccentric childhood and how he became obsessed with China.

**In a Similar Vein:**

* ***River Town*** by Peter Hessler: A modern classic about another young American teaching English in a remote Chinese city.
* ***American Shaolin*** by Matthew Polly: A much more irreverent and humorous look at a Westerner training at the Shaolin Temple.
