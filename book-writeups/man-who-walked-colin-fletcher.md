# XX — The Man Who Walked Through Time

### *Colin Fletcher*

**"I had entered a world where time was measured not in minutes but in the slow wearing away of stone."** — *Colin Fletcher*

---

### **[ The Essentials ]**

* **Genre:** Travel Memoir / Nature
* **Sub-Genre:** Solo Expedition
* **Keywords:** *Grand Canyon, Backpacking, Geology, Solitude, 1963, Survival, Time*
* **Traveler Profile:** **The Purist.** You believe that the only way to understand a landscape is to carry your life on your back and measure the world in footsteps.

---

### **[ The Author: Colin Fletcher (1922–2007) ]**

Colin Fletcher was the father of modern backpacking. He was a British soldier who moved to California and found his true home in the wilderness. He was a man of incredible discipline and a very dry wit. He didn't just hike. He lived in the wild for months at a time. He wrote with a technical precision that changed the way people thought about the outdoors. He was the one who taught a generation how to pack a bag and disappear into the trees. He was a philosopher who used a walking stick as his primary tool.

### **[ The Book: *The Man Who Walked Through Time* ]**

In 1963, Fletcher did something that most people thought was impossible. He became the first person to walk the entire length of Grand Canyon National Park in one continuous journey. He spent two months below the rim. He was completely alone. He had to arrange for food drops and find water in one of the most vertical and unforgiving landscapes on Earth.

The writing is direct and deeply immersive. Fletcher doesn't just tell you about the canyon. He takes you into it. He describes the physical torture of the heat and the constant climbing. But the real story is about the "time" mentioned in the title. As he walks deeper into the canyon, he realizes he is walking through the history of the world. Each layer of rock represents millions of years. He describes the feeling of being a tiny, temporary speck of life moving through an ancient, stone clock.

He uses small paragraphs to capture the quiet moments. He writes about a white-footed mouse that shared his camp and the way the light changed the color of the cliffs from red to gold to deep purple. He has a dramatic flair for describing the silence. It wasn't just a lack of noise. It was a heavy, physical presence. He captures the transition from being a city man to being a creature of the desert. By the end of the walk, he is no longer an intruder. He is part of the landscape.

### **[ Destinations Explored ]**

* **The Inner Gorge:** The deep, dark basement of the canyon where the rocks are nearly two billion years old.
* **Havasu Canyon:** A hidden paradise of turquoise water and lush greenery that felt like a dream after weeks of dust.
* **The Tonto Platform:** The wide, baking terrace of heat where he spent much of his time navigating the side canyons.
* **The Colorado River:** The powerful, brown snake of water that carved the whole magnificent mess.

---

### **[ Further Reading ]**

**By Fletcher:**

* ***The Complete Walker***: The "bible" of backpacking. It is a technical manual that reads like a conversation with a wise, grumpy uncle.
* ***The Thousand-Mile Summer***: His account of walking the entire length of California.

**In a Similar Vein:**

* ***Desert Solitaire*** by Edward Abbey: A much angrier and more political look at the American Southwest.
* ***The Emerald Mile*** by Kevin Fedarko: A dramatic account of the fastest boat ride through the Grand Canyon during a massive flood.

### **[ The Perfect Pairing ]**

* **The Drink:** A mouthful of water filtered from a muddy pothole that tastes faintly of minerals and life.
* **The Soundtrack:** The rhythmic clicking of a walking stick on limestone and the occasional roar of a distant rapid.
* **The Setting:** A small ledge a thousand feet above the river as the sun disappears and the shadows fill the gorge like ink.
