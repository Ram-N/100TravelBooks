# XX — The Road to Oxiana

### *In Search of Oxiana*

**"To go to Persia is to find your own past, live."** — *Robert Byron*

---

### **[ The Essentials ]**

* **Genre:** Architectural Travel Memoir  
* **Sub-Genre:** Middle Eastern Journey, Interwar Exploration  
* **Keywords:** *Persia, Afghanistan, Islamic architecture, diary, eccentric, ruins, borders, interwar Middle East*  
* **Traveler Profile:** **The Aesthetic Pilgrim.** For readers who relish sharp-eyed descriptions of buildings, tart social commentary, and the pleasures and irritations of moving slowly overland through complex frontiers.  

---

### **[ The Author: Robert Byron (1905–1941) ]**

Robert Byron was an English travel writer, art critic, and historian, best known for *The Road to Oxiana*. Educated at Eton and Oxford, he emerged in the 1920s as a brilliant, acerbic voice, publishing early travel books on Greece and Mount Athos before ranging further to India, the Soviet Union, and Tibet. A passionate advocate for Byzantine and Islamic architecture, he campaigned to preserve historic buildings and helped found the Georgian Group, reflecting his conviction that good architecture was a living inheritance, not a museum piece. *The Road to Oxiana* distilled his interests in art, politics, and landscape into one idiosyncratic masterpiece before his early death in 1941, when the ship carrying him on a wartime mission sank off Cape Wrath.  

---

### **[ The Book: *The Road to Oxiana* ]**

*The Road to Oxiana* is framed as a diary of an eleven‑month journey that begins in Venice in August 1933 and runs via Cyprus, Palestine, Syria, Iraq, and Iran to Afghanistan, ending in Peshawar in June 1934. Traveling often with his friend Christopher Sykes, Byron moves by ship, bus, lorry, animal, and occasionally a battered automobile, detouring repeatedly in pursuit of specific mosques, mausoleums, and towers—above all the “Oxiana” region along the Amu Darya on Afghanistan’s northern frontier. Bureaucratic obstruction, arbitrary border rules, arrests over permits, sickness, and sheer bad luck constantly derail the plan, and part of the book’s poignancy is that he never actually reaches the river itself.  

What sets the book apart is not the itinerary but the voice. Byron alternates dense, rapturous readings of buildings in Isfahan, Yazd, or Herat with waspish sketches of colonial officials, fellow travelers, and local elites, mixing high aesthetic judgment with gossip and farce. The diary form allows abrupt shifts in tone—from boredom in bad lodgings to sudden revelation before a tiled dome—mirroring the rhythms of long overland travel. Written in the tense interwar years, it also doubles as an oblique political document, capturing a Middle East and Central Asia poised between old empires, new nation‑states, and approaching war. For later writers, *Oxiana* became a touchstone for how travel narrative could fuse rigorous art history with irreverent, modernist prose.  

---

### **[ Destinations Explored ]**

* **Isfahan (Esfahan), Iran:** A centerpiece of the journey, where Byron studies Safavid mosques and squares, treating the city as a pinnacle of Islamic urban design.  
* **Yazd and central Iranian towns:** Desert cities whose wind towers, shrines, and caravanserais offer a counterpoint to Isfahan’s grandeur and test the traveler’s endurance.  
* **Herat, Afghanistan:** A long‑coveted goal for Byron, with Timurid monuments and tilework that he sees as crucial to understanding the evolution of Islamic architecture.  
* **Jerusalem, Damascus, and Baghdad:** Key Levantine and Mesopotamian waypoints that provide mosques, churches, bazaars, and political tension as the journey winds east.  
* **Oxiana (around the Amu Darya):** The elusive region that gives the book its title, representing not only a geographical objective but an almost mythic horizon that he never quite attains.  

---

### **[ Further Reading ]**

**By Robert Byron:**  

* ***The Station: Athos***: An earlier account of a pilgrimage to the monastic republic of Mount Athos, already showing his fusion of architectural detail, religious history, and sardonic observation.  

**In a Similar Vein:**  

* ***Seven Pillars of Wisdom*** by T. E. Lawrence: A dense, reflective narrative of wartime travels and insurgency in the Arab Revolt, mixing landscape, politics, and interior drama.  
* ***From the Holy Mountain*** by William Dalrymple: A late‑20th‑century retracing of Byzantine Christian routes through the Levant and Turkey, echoing Byron’s blend of history, architecture, and contemporary reportage.  
* ***The Marsh Arabs*** by Wilfred Thesiger: An immersive portrait of southern Iraq’s wetlands and their inhabitants, offering another mid‑century view of the region’s margins.

### **[ The Signature Line ]**

> “Oxiana” here is less a place than a mirage at the edge of maps, and the real destination of Byron’s journey is a way of seeing buildings, landscapes, and people with unsparing clarity.

### **[ The Perfect Pairing ]**

* **The Drink:** Strong black tea served in small glasses, or thick Turkish coffee sipped slowly as if in a roadside café between buses.  
* **The Soundtrack:** A mix of Persian classical music and Levantine oud improvisations, with occasional radio static or street noise to evoke interwar city soundscapes.  
* **The Setting:** A quiet table by a window in an old café or library, with a map of the Middle East and Central Asia to trace the zigzagging route as you read.
