# A Small Place

**"An ugly thing, that is what you are when you become a tourist."** — *Jamaica Kincaid*

### **[ The Essentials ]**

* **Genre:** Extended Essay / Cultural Critique
* **Sub-Genre:** Postcolonial Literature, Anti-Tourism Manifesto
* **Keywords:** *Colonialism, Rage, Tourism, Antigua, Corruption, Injustice, Complicity, Caribbean*
* **Traveler Profile:** **The Uncomfortable Truth-Seeker.** For the reader willing to be told they are the problem—that their vacation perpetuates the exploitation they claim to abhor.

---

### **[ The Author: Jamaica Kincaid (b. 1949) ]**

Born Elaine Potter Richardson in St. John's, Antigua, Jamaica Kincaid left her island home at sixteen to work as an au pair in New York, where she reinvented herself entirely. She changed her name in 1973, severing ties with a family that disapproved of her writing, and found her voice at *The New Yorker*, where she wrote for two decades. Her prose—lyrical, furious, uncompromising—emerged from the dual wounds of British colonialism and maternal betrayal, twin themes that haunt her work. By the time *A Small Place* appeared in 1988, Kincaid had already established herself with *At the Bottom of the River* and *Annie John*, but this slim volume marked a new register: pure, undiluted rage. The book was so incendiary that her own editor at *The New Yorker* refused to publish it, and Kincaid was reportedly banned from returning to Antigua for years. Now a professor at Harvard and one of the Caribbean's most essential literary voices, she continues to write from Vermont, still wielding language like a scalpel.

### **[ The Book: *A Small Place* ]**

This is not a travelogue. It is an indictment. In just eighty-one pages, Kincaid dissects the ten-by-twelve-mile island where she grew up, addressing the reader—you, the tourist—with devastating directness. The book opens with your arrival at the V.C. Bird International Airport, named for a corrupt Prime Minister, and follows you through the island's decay: the dilapidated hospital where ministers go abroad for care, the earthquake-damaged library left in ruins for decades, the Japanese cars careening over potholed roads. The clear blue water you came to photograph? It hides the sewage from your hotel. The beauty you seek is built on the poverty you refuse to see.

Written in four sections, *A Small Place* moves from savage satire to historical reckoning. Kincaid traces Antigua's journey from British colony (independence came only in 1981) to a present marked by governmental corruption that mirrors the very imperialism it supposedly escaped. She writes with biblical cadence and Swiftian fury, her sentences building in incantatory waves. The prose has the hypnotic quality of her fiction but stripped of any cushioning narrative—this is pure confrontation. Kincaid refuses easy absolution: not for the British who enslaved her ancestors, not for the Antiguan officials who pillage their own people, and certainly not for the well-meaning tourist who thinks a nice vacation is apolitical.

What makes *A Small Place* more than a polemic is its emotional complexity. Kincaid mourns the Antigua of her childhood even as she acknowledges the colonial manipulation embedded in her nostalgia. She writes in English—"the language of the criminal"—to describe crimes for which English offers no adequate terms. This is not objective reportage but a daughter's reckoning with a homeland that can never be home again, a place simultaneously beloved and unbearable.

### **[ Destinations Explored ]**

* **Antigua:** A small Caribbean island reduced to a postcard fantasy for tourists, its post-independence reality marked by institutional decay and government corruption.
* **The Library (destroyed 1974):** Once a refuge and symbol of education, now a crumbling metaphor for neglect, sporting a sign that reads "Repairs are Pending."
* **Mill Reef Club:** An exclusive resort where wealthy whites recreate colonial leisure while native Antiguans serve their comfort.

---

### **[ Further Reading ]**

**By Jamaica Kincaid:**

* ***Annie John***: Her semi-autobiographical novel of girlhood in Antigua, before the fury—tender and devastating in equal measure.
* ***Lucy***: A young woman from the Caribbean works as an au pair in America, navigating displacement and self-discovery with Kincaid's characteristic sharpness.

**In a Similar Vein:**

* ***The Fire Next Time*** by James Baldwin: Another slim volume that burns with righteous anger at systemic injustice, written in prose that demands to be read aloud.
* ***How Europe Underdeveloped Africa*** by Walter Rodney: For those ready to understand the economic structures behind the moral fury Kincaid expresses.

### **[ The Perfect Pairing ]**

* **The Drink:** Strong black coffee, no sugar—bitter and clarifying.
* **The Soundtrack:** The sound of waves against a dock, underlaid with the distant hum of generators keeping hotel lights burning while neighborhoods go dark.
* **The Setting:** Anywhere you are comfortable enough to be made deeply uncomfortable—this book should disrupt your peace.
