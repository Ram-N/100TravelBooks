# Travels with Charley

### *In Search of America*

**"I was writing of something I did not know about, and it seems to me that in a so-called writer this is criminal."** — *John Steinbeck*

### **[ The Author: John Steinbeck (1902–1968) ]**

Born February 27, 1902, in Salinas, California, he remains the quintessential California writer—son of a flour mill manager, raised among farmworkers, educated fitfully at Stanford (never graduated), shaped by the Salinas Valley and Monterey County that would anchor his greatest works. He spent the 1920s laboring and failing in New York before returning to California, where *Tortilla Flat* (1935) brought first success, followed by *In Dubious Battle* (1936), *Of Mice and Men* (1937), and *The Grapes of Wrath* (1939)—the Pulitzer Prize-winning masterpiece that made him simultaneously beloved and controversial for its Depression-era sympathies and critique of capitalism. His hometown of Salinas called him communist; migrant workers called him witness. World War II transformed him into war correspondent. The 1940s brought marine biology collaborations with Ed Ricketts (*Sea of Cortez*), establishing him as an early ecological thinker. *East of Eden* (1952)—his ambitious Salinas Valley family saga—aimed to reclaim stature but divided critics. By 1960, Steinbeck was fifty-eight, living in New York and Sag Harbor with his third wife Elaine, sensing disconnection from the country he wrote about. "I was writing of something I did not know about," he admitted—a criminal offense for a writer. He felt soft, depressed, recently diagnosed with heart trouble. So he bought a GMC pickup, fitted it with a custom camper, named it Rocinante after Don Quixote's horse, and departed after Labor Day with his wife's ten-year-old French poodle Charley. The journey lasted approximately eleven weeks, covering 10,000 miles around America's perimeter. The book appeared July 1962. Months later, he received the Nobel Prize in Literature. The New York Times editorial page questioned whether a writer with a 1930s moral vision deserved it. Wounded, unwell, disillusioned by America's greed and his own sons' behavior, Steinbeck wrote no more fiction. He died December 20, 1968, in New York City, of heart disease. His ashes rest in Salinas.

### **[ The Book: *Travels with Charley* ]**

The setup is simple: aging novelist, feeling estranged from his subject matter, travels America with a poodle to rediscover the country. The reality is complicated. Published as nonfiction, the book topped bestseller lists and became an American classic—beloved for its humane observations, its gentle humor about Charley (who prefers negotiation to fighting, has a roar like a lion to hide his cowardice, and likes getting up early to make Steinbeck rise too), and its melancholy portrait of 1960 America on the cusp of tumultuous change.

The route follows America's outer border: Long Island to Maine, through the Midwest to Montana (with which he falls in love), to Seattle, down to his native Salinas Valley, across to Texas (where he stays at ranches), to New Orleans (witnessing school desegregation protests that shock him), then through the Deep South and back to New York—where, ironically, he gets lost and must ask directions home. The prose operates in Steinbeck's mature register: philosophical, observant, occasionally mystical. He describes the giant redwoods inspiring awe in even the vainest men. He marvels at American restlessness—"nearly every American hungers to move"—recognizing his own wanderlust as national character. He worries about environmental destruction, population shifts, racial tensions, technological change leaving the country directionless and endangered.

The book's most famous sequence occurs in New Orleans, where Steinbeck observes the "Cheerleaders"—white women screaming abuse at Black children integrating William Frantz Elementary School. The scene disturbs him profoundly, revealing America's ugliness beneath its prosperity. Throughout, he uses Charley as conversational foil, exploring thoughts through imagined dialogue with the dog—a novelist's device masquerading as travelogue technique.

Then comes the controversy. In 2010, journalist Bill Steigerwald retraced Steinbeck's route exactly fifty years later and discovered extensive fabrication. Letters Steinbeck wrote during the trip revealed he wasn't traveling alone—his wife Elaine joined him for forty-five of seventy-five days. They stayed at luxury hotels, resorts, and the Steinbeck family cottage in Pacific Grove, not rough camping sites. The famous encounter with an itinerant Shakespearean actor in North Dakota? Steinbeck's letters place him 350 miles away that day. Dialogue reads prose-perfect because Steinbeck invented it. Steinbeck's own son later confirmed: "He just sat in his camper and wrote all that [expletive]."

Steigerwald's exposé forced Penguin to finally acknowledge in 2012 that the book contained "countless touches—changing the sequence of events, elaborating on scenes, inventing dialogue—that one associates more with fiction than nonfiction." Steinbeck scholars counter that writers shape material, that essential truth matters more than factual accuracy, that Steinbeck was "at heart a novelist" using everything in his arsenal to create readable, vivid narrative. The debate continues: Is it creative nonfiction ahead of its time, or dishonest fraud? The book remains in print, beloved despite—or perhaps because of—its complicated relationship with truth. What's undeniable: it captures something real about American longing, about feeling lost in your own country, about the gap between the nation you remember and the one that exists.

### **[ Destinations Explored ]**

* **Maine Coast:** Where America's perimeter begins, and Steinbeck starts feeling the country's texture again after years of New York abstraction.
* **Montana:** The place he falls unexpectedly in love with—wide, empty, beautiful, representing an America not yet paved over.
* **Salinas Valley, California:** His birthplace, revisited after twenty years, unrecognizable from population growth and progress.
* **New Orleans:** Where he witnesses the desegregation of William Frantz Elementary School and encounters the "Cheerleaders" screaming at Black children—America's ugliest moment in the journey.
* **The Giant Redwoods:** Where even irreverent men fall silent in wonder, and where Charley famously refuses to urinate on the trees (which Steinbeck interprets as either deep respect or elaborate joke).

### **[ Further Reading ]**

**By John Steinbeck:**

* ***The Grapes of Wrath***: His 1939 Pulitzer Prize-winning masterpiece—if *Charley* is his late-career attempt to reconnect with America, this is the book that defined his relationship with it.
* ***East of Eden***: His 1952 ambitious saga of the Salinas Valley, which he called an "autobiography" of his homeland—essential for understanding what he returned to find changed.

**In a Similar Vein:**

* ***Blue Highways*** by William Least Heat-Moon: The 1982 spiritual successor—another man, another vehicle, another quest to find America on back roads.
* ***Dogging Steinbeck*** by Bill Steigerwald: The 2012 exposé that proved the fabrication but also captured contemporary America—essential counterpoint for readers wanting the full story.

### **[ The Perfect Pairing ]**

* **The Drink:** Coffee in a thermos, consumed while parked somewhere beautiful, contemplating the gap between memory and present reality.
* **The Soundtrack:** The hum of a GMC pickup truck engine, punctuated by Charley's occasional "Ftt" when he needs his evening biscuits and walk.
* **The Setting:** A custom camper named after a fictional horse, containing enough comfort to make roughing it bearable and enough isolation to feel alone—even when you're not.
