## 2. Core Book Structure (macro-level)

You are already thinking in the right direction with 1–2 pages per book. I would suggest:

### A. Front Matter

* Short introduction: *What is a travel book?*
* How the list was chosen (no rankings, no “best,” emphasis on range and voice)
* How to use the book (dip-in reading, discovery, rereading)

### B. The 100 Books (main body)

* Alphabetical by author **or**
* Loosely grouped by category (with cross-tags noted per book)

### C. Interstitial Essays (every 8–12 books)

Short 1–2 page essays that reset the reader before the next batch.

### D. Back Matter

* Index by region
* Index by theme
* Index by author
* “If you liked X, read Y” map

## 4. Interstitial Essays (very important)

These will give the book its *shape* and keep it from feeling like a list.

Possible essay topics:

### A. “The Great Travel Writers”

Short portraits of:

* **Paul Theroux** – restlessness, observation, friction
* **Pico Iyer** – stillness, exile, belonging
* **Peter Matthiessen** – nature, discipline, spirituality
* **Bruce Chatwin** – myth, movement, obsession

These don’t need to be biographies. Focus on:

* What kind of traveler they are
* What they notice that others miss
* Why their books endure

### B. “Why Travel Writing Is Not Escapism”

Travel books as:

* Social documents
* Moral records
* Acts of attention

### C. “The Ethics of Travel Writing”

* The outsider’s gaze
* Privilege and access
* Then vs now
* Writing about poverty, conflict, and difference

### D. “Rereading Places”

Writers who return:

* Same place, different self
* How memory changes geography

## 5. Per-Book Page Template (very workable)

For each book, consider a consistent structure:

1. **Title, Author, Year**
2. **Where and when the journey happens**
3. **What kind of journey this is**
4. **Why this book matters**
5. **Who should read it**
6. Optional: a short quote (1–2 lines)

This keeps things readable and prevents over-writing.

---

## 6. Cross-Referencing (adds real value)

At the bottom of each entry:

* “If you liked this, also try…”
* “Pairs well with…”
* “A counterpoint to…”

This turns the book into a *map*, not a list.

## 7. What Makes Your Book Distinct

From everything you’ve said, your differentiators are:

* Breadth without superficiality
* Calm, reflective tone
* No ranking anxiety
* Respect for rereading
* Travel as a lifelong practice, not bucket lists

You are not telling people *where to go*.
